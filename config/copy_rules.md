# Nooch Copy Rules

The single source of truth for **all Nooch copy** (website first, later also email and
social). This document is human-owned and meant to be enriched over time. The content
skills read it; the human curates it.

Distilled from: StoryBrand "Grow a Pair", the Buyer Personas, the Personal Gain Angles,
Sell the Sizzle (Futerra), Chase Dimond's copywriting lessons, the customer update
emails, and the language-as-system-change note (JW).

## 0. How to use this

- **Working decisions (2026-06-23):**
  - Public content is written in **English** by default (the village's working language).
    The buying market is Dutch, so Dutch versions may follow per piece; never mix languages
    inside one piece.
  - **One persona per piece.** The brief names the single reader. Don't write for everyone.
  - This document is the basis for **all** Nooch copy; we start with website content.
- **The writing loop (human in the middle):** the LLM drafts a full first draft against
  these rules → a **human rewrites** it in their judgement and voice → the LLM does a
  final check against these rules and the claim-gate. Every text passes a human.
- **Internal vs external voice (two regimes).** This filter governs **outward-facing** copy
  only. Internal village communications (Field Notes, the daily bulletin, exec summaries) are
  written in the **voice of the role-holder's persona** (e.g. Corry Coconut's plain factual
  tone, Noochie's warm ENFP), not in this brand voice. Only when content goes **public** does
  it pass through these rules. The boundary is simply: does this leave the village?

## 1. Who we talk to (pick ONE per piece)

The StoryBrand character: *someone whose world just got a little bigger.* They weren't
researching sustainable fashion. They saw how Nooch shoes are made and thought "wait,
this exists?" Not converted, just informed. They can't unsee it now.

Three primary personas (the brief picks one):

- **Mark — The Convinced** (Euromonitor: Empowered Activist). Already sold on
  sustainability; stress-testing us. Needs **proof and credibility, not warmth**. His
  content looks like **evidence, not inspiration**. "Small business / made by real people"
  beats "sustainability". Reflect him back to himself; don't convince him. He is our
  multiplier (word-of-mouth converts ~8x our influencer rate).
- **Yasmine — The Doubter** (Euromonitor: Undaunted Striver, style-conscious). Filters on
  **aesthetics, not sustainability**. She's frozen, not unconvinced. **Win the design
  conversation first**; leading with the green story actively hurts (green claims can lower
  perceived fashion image). She needs to see **someone like her wearing them** in a context
  she recognises. Price ceiling ~€150.
- **Noor — The Idealist** (Euromonitor: Minimalist Seeker). Already wants to buy; it's a
  **price gap, not a belief gap**. Close it with **perceived value, not discounts** (e.g. a
  personal impact figure: "your pair, 4.2 kg CO₂, one pair of hands paid fairly"). She
  doesn't want to preach; she wants **the shoes to preach for her** ("where did you get
  those?").

### Reader temperaments (MBTI / Keirsey) and page architecture

The v2.0 persona sets *who* a piece is for and its angle (pick one). The temperaments below
are a second lens about *how to lay out the page*: a single page is read by all four
temperaments at once, with conflicting wants. Don't pick one, **order the page** so each is
served in turn. Web readers scan rather than read, so use short paragraphs, bullets, bold
and white space throughout.

The four temperaments (with our segment share):

- **Rationals (NT) — "is this the smartest choice?"** (~5%, the Fast Decision-Maker). Write
  the why, the logic, specs, comparisons. Tone: objective, concise, a little challenging.
  Format: feature comparisons, tight logic.
- **Artisans (SP) — "what do I get, and now?"** (~35%, the Spontaneous Buyer). Write the
  immediate benefit, "how it works", something to do. Tone: energetic, direct, pragmatic.
  Format: short sentences, bold CTA, white space, imagery.
- **Idealists (NF) — "what does this mean?"** (~15%, the People-Person). Write the mission,
  stories of impact, community. Tone: warm, inspiring, authentic. Format: storytelling,
  vivid and metaphorical language.
- **Guardians (SJ) — "is this proven and safe?"** (~40%, the Researcher). Write step-by-step,
  guarantees, testimonials, proof. Tone: respectful, practical, organised. Format: numbered
  lists, logical sequence.

**Page architecture (this resolves the conflicting interests):**

- **Above the fold: serve NT + SP first.** They are the impatient ones and bounce if not
  hooked instantly. Lead with a sharp hook, the immediate benefit, the one smart reason, and
  a clear CTA.
- **Below the fold: then NF, then SJ.** Once the impatient are hooked, give the meaning and
  story (NF) and the proof, testimonials and structure (SJ) for those who scroll down to
  decide.

So one page serves opposing readers by sequence: the fast and logical up top, the deep and
proof-seeking below.

## 2. The desired outcome & the spine (StoryBrand "Grow a Pair")

Before writing, name the **emotion** the reader should feel and the **transformation** they
seek. Then write toward it.

- **Problem:** most shoes are made from oil (plastic, foam, synthetic rubber). Not a moral
  failure, just a gap in the information, a design problem that hadn't been solved.
- **Guide (Nooch):** we grow shoes. 100% plant-based, made to order, designed to return to
  the earth. The better option, stated plainly.
- **Plan:** Seed (pre-order) → Grow (production at 500, backstage pass) → Harvest (arrives)
  → Wear → Return.
- **Call:** Grow a pair. Join the harvest.
- **Success:** you now know something most people don't; the old version stops making sense.
- **Cost of status quo:** nothing dramatic, you just keep buying oil shoes, unaware there
  was another option.

## 3. Tone of Voice — the four pillars

> We are the better option that didn't need to announce itself. No preaching, no urgency,
> no guilt. A cleaner design, stated plainly and playfully, for people who can see it.

- **Playful Rebellion** (the "Grow a Pair" attitude). Cheeky, slightly provocative, never
  angry. Do: *"We grow shoes. Because wrapping your feet in crude oil derivatives is weird
  once you think about it."* Don't: *"Stop wearing plastic and save the planet!"*
- **The Open Door.** Show something they didn't know existed; let the logic do the work.
  Inform, don't convert. Do: *"Most sneakers are made from crude oil derivatives. Ours
  aren't. Here's what we use instead."* Don't: *"You can make a difference with every step."*
- **The Judo Move.** Never attack the industry. State the old norm as a dry, slightly
  outdated fact and place our solution next to it without commentary. Do: *"The industry
  calls a warehouse full of unsold shoes standard practice. We only make what's ordered."*
  Don't: *"Fast fashion is destroying the planet."*
- **Earthy & Patient.** The wait is proof, not a bug. Own it without apology and make it
  entertaining. Do: *"We make your shoes after you order them. That takes a few months.
  That's the point."* Don't: apologise for lead times.

## 4. The four checks (every piece passes these)

1. **The Smirk Check.** Does it make you smile or raise a knowing eyebrow? If it reads like
   a tech manual or an angry manifesto, add Playful Rebellion.
2. **The Contrast Principle.** Put the old norm next to the Nooch way, neutral language for
   the status quo, let the gap speak.
3. **The Try-Hard Check.** Read it aloud. Does it sound like we're performing or begging for
   approval? Strip it back. Colder, factual, shorter. Confidence needs no padding.
4. **The Mainstream Check.** Would someone who never thinks about sustainable materials find
   it logical? If it only lands with the already-convinced, rewrite it.

## 5. Framing & vocabulary (mission-deep)

- **Citizen, not consumer (the leverage-point rule).** Never address the reader as a
  "consumer". The word implies responsibility begins and ends in the marketplace, the exact
  paradigm Nooch challenges. Words shape paradigms shape systems (Meadows' leverage points);
  using old words reinforces old systems. Address them as a person, a maker, a pioneer.
  Aligns with the lexicon (`consumer_frame` = avoid, `burger_frame` preferred).
- **Trust words vs SEO words.** In the Dutch market, "fair production", "no plastics" and
  "no chemicals" (and "recyclable", ~53% trust) outperform "vegan" and "biodegradable"
  (~38%). Rule: lead with **no plastic / fair / no chemicals** for persuasion; use
  **vegan / plant-based** for findability (SEO), not as the headline claim.
- **Sell the sizzle, not the sausage.** Open with a **desirable vision**, not data. Sell
  heaven, not hell (a vivid positive picture is more believable, the availability
  heuristic). Vision first wins the right to attention; cut dates and percentages from the
  vision (they belong in the plan).
- **No guilt, no fear, no urgency** as levers. Nooch wins by making the better option
  obvious, not by scaring people.

## 6. Structure & frameworks

- **Most copy follows one pattern:** Headline → Lead → Body → Offer → CTA.
- **Slippery slope:** the headline sells the subheadline, which sells the lead, which sells
  the body, which sells the CTA. Each part builds curiosity for the next.
- **The headline is ~80% of the effort.** If it doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else gets
  read. Always produce **several headline options** to choose from.
- **Rule of One:** one reader, one big idea, one CTA. Too many offers = confused readers.
- **WIIFM / "you" not "we".** People care about themselves; aim for a "you" : "we/I" ratio
  of at least 2:1.
- **Never start from scratch.** Use a proven frame and pick per piece: **PAS**
  (Problem-Agitate-Solve), **AIDA** (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action), **FAB**
  (Features-Advantages-Benefits), or Hook-Story-Close.

## 7. WIIFM library (personal-gain angles to draw on)

The reader cares about themselves first, then the planet. Personal benefits to lean on:

- **Your body, not just the planet.** Synthetic textiles shed microplastics that the skin
  absorbs (more so when warm and sweating). Plant-based touches your skin without the plastic.
- **The at-peace / helper's-high feeling.** Choosing not to use animals/oil triggers a real
  reward response (oxytocin, serotonin, dopamine); giving benefits the giver.
- **Comfort & quality.** Natural rubber / crepe sole: shock absorption, support, grip,
  durability, and it biodegrades, unlike TPU/EVA.

## 8. Language level

- Public web pages: **B1–B2** by default (clear, short sentences, small words over big ones).
  Pillar/explainer pages may go to B2/C1 where the reader (e.g. Mark) expects depth.
- Always: short sentences, write as you talk, tell the truth, end paragraphs with a hook.

## 9. SEO rules (findability)

- Category terms (barefoot, sustainable, vegan, eco-friendly shoes) are how people **find**
  us, use them for discovery and in metadata/headings.
- Mission terms (plastic-free, regenerative) have little search volume, you **create** demand
  for them in the content by borrowing the category term's volume to introduce the mission.
- Don't sacrifice the trust-framing (section 5) for an SEO keyword; SEO gets you found, the
  framing earns the read.

## 10. Claim & legal rules

- Only **verified** knowledge cards may be used as a **hard claim**, especially in
  marketing/sales pages and the product passport (the publication gate enforces this).
- Forbidden as positive words in sales copy: "plastic", "leer" (leather).
- CO₂ figures, biodegradability and comparative claims need a real, cited source, never
  asserted on vibes.

## 11. What does NOT work (anti-patterns)

- Preaching, guilt-tripping, doom, urgency, "save the planet" calls to action.
- Attacking the industry or competitors.
- Generic green-speak that only lands with the already-convinced.
- Calling the reader a "consumer".
- Apologising for the wait, or promising faster delivery.
- Try-hard, padded, performing copy. If unsure, cut 30%.

## 12. Community & email voice (distilled from the real customer updates)

Warm, radically transparent, human. Transparency is the brand, not a tactic. This is where
the Open Door, Earthy & Patient and Playful Rebellion pillars become concrete. The rules:

1. **Radical transparency, including bad news.** Share problems openly and early ("there's a
   problem on our path") instead of going quiet. Silence breaks trust faster than bad news.
2. **Share your own feelings.** Be vulnerable ("we're disappointed and proud at the same
   time"). The makers are people, not a faceless brand.
3. **Tie every setback back to the why.** "Every time, we remember why we started: the
   polluting shoe industry has to change." A delay becomes proof of the mission.
4. **Name the tribe warmly, and let it grow.** "Supporters", "pioneers", "fans from the very
   first hour". The reader is a member, not a buyer.
5. **Make the reader a co-conspirator.** "Your support makes this revolution possible." They
   enable the change; they're not just paying for a product.
6. **Radical accessibility.** "Ask us anything", a real phone number, sign personally (Lotte
   and Stefan). The Open Door, literally.
7. **Backstage over polished.** Share the messy real thing (the factory video, honest detail
   about materials like PLIANT and Mirum), not slick marketing.
8. **Celebrate playfully, even in delay.** "FIN-A-LLY", the odd emoji, "YES!!!". Warmth and
   fun carry the hard news.
9. **Acknowledge and thank the community's response.** "Our inbox exploded with supportive
   messages, a huge boost." Reflect the relationship back to them.
